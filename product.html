A Good Girl's Guide To Murder by Holly Jackson

A Good Girl’s Guide to Murder by Holly Jackson is a young adult mystery novel that follows Pippa Fitz-Amobi, a high school student who investigates a closed murder case for her school project. As Pip digs deeper, she begins to uncover secrets that suggest the case may not be as simple as everyone believes. The story combines suspense, clever investigation, and unexpected twists, making it an engaging read about truth, justice, and curiosity.

No Longer Human by Dazai Osamu

Rp70.000

No Longer Human (original Japanese title: Ningen Shikkaku) is a classic novel by Japanese author Osamu Dazai, first published in 1948. It’s widely regarded as one of the most important works of post-war Japanese literature, and has remained influential and widely read across generations and cultures. The story is presented as a series of personal writings by the protagonist, Ōba Yōzō, who struggles to understand himself and others. Feeling disconnected from society and unable to show his true feelings, Yōzō learns to wear a “mask” of superficial charm and humor, hiding deep inner turmoil. The novel explores themes of identity, alienation, the search for meaning, and what it means to be human. This powerful and introspective work has resonated with many readers for its raw reflection on the human experience.

The Bell Jar by Sylvia Plath

Rp.258.000

The Bell Jar is the only novel by American writer Sylvia Plath, first published in 1963 under a pseudonym. Set in the 1950s, the story follows Esther Greenwood, a young woman who wins a summer internship at a New York magazine but soon finds herself struggling with her sense of identity, societal expectations, and emotional balance. Feeling increasingly disconnected from the world and pressured by rigid norms about what her life should be, Esther slips into a challenging period of anxiety and deep emotional struggle. The title metaphor—the “bell jar”—symbolizes the feeling of being trapped under an invisible barrier, observing life while being unable to fully participate in it. The novel is both introspective and literary, exploring themes of identity, freedom, pressure, and self-understanding through Esther’s voice. It remains an influential work in modern literature for its honest portrayal of a young person questioning her place in the world.
